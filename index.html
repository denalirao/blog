Marruecos / Morocco

23 Feb. 2015

Last weekend, I went on a trip (excursion, actually) to Morocco. The excursion put us up in a super nice hotel that felt kinda insulating...but also was very nice. We ate dinner there the first night, and they had traditional dancers come in ( Sort of like this ).

My first impressions of Morocco outside of the hotel were on a bus ride to the city of Chefchauen, and were mostly of the scenery. It was scenic - fog, classic rolling medium green hills that looked less touched by development/farming than those of Spain, dotted with the cutest livestock I've seen anywhere. The sheep, the goats, even the stray dogs seemed more liberated. It was also nice to see un-manicured grass, one thing I miss in Sevilla.

Chefchauen itself struck me as both fun and exciting and remarkably peacful. All the walls are painted blue to repel insects, and the doors of houses are color coded for the inhabitants - blue for Andalusian (moors who left spain), green and grey for Berbers, brown for Jews. The streets are too small for cars - instead, donkeys with 5-gallon tanks strapped to their backs plod up and down the brick/mud roads. The word Chefchauen means (approximately) "look at the peaks," because the city is surrounded by three mountains. These were pretty and supposedly have great hiking trails, which we unfortunately didn't have time for. The waterfall on the inside edge of the city walls was big, but peaceful, in keeping with the city's character.


After lunch, we wandered up and down streets and between markets, working up the courage to haggle. Halfway through our collective first attempt, we realized that though our guides had taught us the rudiments of haggling, they hadn't given us the faintest idea of the going price for anything. We were reduced to guessing, and alternated between offending people with low offers and raising eyebrows with high ones. The statuettes, leather purses, scarves, bracelets, etc. were the same in every street stall, which made it easier.

Most of the transactions we carried out in Spanish. Some of the merchants spoke English, some didn't and only spoke Arabic, French, and Spanish. Speaking Spanish to the Moroccan merchants went more smoothly than it often does with Spaniards, because there was an understanding that this was a second language for both of us (well, my second; their third). The ones we hadn't already offended with too-low offers were friendly, happy to converse, and patient with our tenuous grasp on the haggling process.

Very soon into our wanderings, it became hard to ignore an interesting phenomenon. Moroccans were really excited that I was Indian. Every fourth street vendor would spot me peering into stalls and exclaim "India!" And I would agree, taken aback by their enthusiasm and unsure of the proper response, and they would stop eveything they were doing to recite names of Bollywood actors. One guy listed off 7 (we both counted on our fingers), and one started singing and dancing. One bowed, raised his hands as for a namaste, and gravely said Kuch Kuch Hota Hai. I passed one man and we had the conversation I was still confused by but by this point accustomed to; India--Si--Shahrukh Khan!--Si! I later passed him again and said "Shahrukh Khan!" and he grinned from ear to ear, high-fived me, and responded in kind "Amitabh Bhachan!" I finally had time for a longer conversation with one of these India enthusiasts and asked him why Moroccans were such admirers of Shahrukh and Amitabh. Bollywood movies are always on tv, he said (in Spanish), very fun, very good for watching with the family. Hollywood movies are too violent and have "mucho sexo", but Bollywood movies, he smiled, are better, happier, and, I inferred, more suited to a Muslim country and values.
