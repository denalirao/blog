30 May 2015

Fin

   Yeah, yeah, it's been another month and a half. Sorry, world. I've been traveling!

My program in Sevilla ended, and since then, I haven't been in the same place for more than five days. I'll try to describe some details of some of these places below, and probably not really capture them, so just know that they were all fascinating in their own way.

I think I'll go chronologically backwards. Most recently, I had a 20-hour layover in Reykjavik, Iceland. This was probably the most different place from anywhere else I've visited, an interesting way to end this 5 months.The IcelandAir flight to Reykjavik had presented a continuous stream of facts about Iceland, one of which was that (1) sheep outnumber people. However, (2), the only native land mammal is the Arctic fox, which leads to the conclusion the net immigration of sheep has been higher than that of humans in the past thousand years. (3), Farming is hard on a volcanic island and until recently Icelanders would only be able to get fruit on Christmas. I got off the plane prepared with these facts, the name of a hostel booking, and as it turned out, not enough warm clothes.

It was much colder than Southern Europe. A chilling wind blew toward the city across the North Sea and from the snow-covered mountains in the near distance. It was much less crowded - Reykjavik has a population of 118,000, the whole of Iceland 323,000. This was clearly evident in the streets, which I had mostly to myself. The architecture was very different from Southern Europe -- austere, concrete, imposing.




It was great. To visit, at least, I think it would be hard to live there. Although, (4), 95% of homes in Iceland are connected to the internet. Anyway, I visited the church pictured above, which was completely unlike the typical Spanish churches with dark gold and religious paintings and candles everywhere. The inside was airy and well lit by lots of skylights, and there were no decorations except for two little sculptures on tables. They were titled 'Sacrifice' and something else, the only clue they were remotely religious.
  I stopped for Icelandic meat soup, which was, as fact number 3 could have suggested, devoid of vegetables except for onions. That night it was light out until 1 in the morning and again at 3:30am. The hostel was luckily equipped with blackout curtains.




12 April 2015

El Balcón

   April 12 - not an auspicious day (as far as I know), but typing it out has brought with it a couple realizations. First, that it's been a while since my last post - oops. Second, that I've been a while in Sevilla. In fact, I'm only here for exactly one more month...

   One of the things I will miss most about Sevilla is my balcony. It's a modest balcony, compared to the others on the street; no ornately molded railings, no overhang, no flowerpots. It measures perhaps 5 feet by 3 feet, with a simple tile floor, and I may have spent more time in it than any single other location in Sevilla. (In a good way, not a sad way.) I have definitely eaten more meals here than anywhere else, partly for the convenience and joy of sweeping the crumbs off and watching them tumble down three stories to land on the street or the overhang of the restaurant below. If you stand up you can see the street and the people passing below, but if you follow my example and drag a chair outside and prop your feet on the railing, your view is of the sky, a lot of television antennaes, two floors each of four other apartment buildings (all with balconies!), and then the leafy green canopy of orange trees covering the ground floor of the other buildings. You can smell the orange flowers up here. Occasionally the smell of food, too, wafts up from the aforementioned restaurant.




26 March 2015

Three Sevillans Walk into a Bar . . .

  About a third of all Sevillans are deeply affected by flamenco. There are often free flamenco shows in bars, and sitting there as the guitar picks up and the clapping finds its rhythm, you can see these people losing themselves in the music. Their eyes close and some sway, some slap their knees, and all of them somehow innately know when to utter an "Olééé...". I haven't been able to figure out any pattern to when they do it, and I thought of asking one of them but have no idea how I would phrase that question.

  Next week is one of Sevilla's most important weeks of the year - Semana Santa, the week leading up to Easter. The government of Sevilla is really excited. The citizens of Sevilla are less so. Let me explain why. Instead of good ol' chocolate bunnies and eggs, every day people dress up in full robes and pointed hats and parade around the streets carrying ornate pallets with the Virgin Mary and sometimes Jesus. It takes 40 people to carry each one of these pasos, and there is one from each church. Sevilla is covered in churches. This means the streets become impossible to walk through and the air is filled with the music and incense carried by each procession until 2 in the morning. This is what it looks like:

There are 40 people under there
This is(was) the main street

The government is really excited for all this because a horrendous number of tourists come to Sevilla. The people are not because the city is held hostage for a week. More than a week, if you consider the preparations. They've been building bleachers for people to sit and watch the processions for the past month, taking up most of many plazas. Streets have been sectioned into pedestrian parts and procession parts (always bigger). Cars are banned. They have paved over their only tram line. Just paved it over with asphalt.

   Also noteworthy: the people who walk in front of the pasos wear full robes and pointed hats, and look pretty much exactly like the KKK. They are not, our professor explained, they do it for anonymity. Someone pointed out that this is exactly why the KKK did it.

    Today I ate lunch leaning over my balcony dropping crumbs on the head of the guy on the street trying to hand out flyers. The top of his head had a couple stringy hairs. From the back of his head issued a ponytail and from the front an endless stream of words -- "Hola chico toma en contra (Hello take this against)" - and then they'd push past him and he'd start again with the next person. He'd stand in their ways, strategically intercept certain people, less strategically hit people with flyers, and they all waved/pushed him off. I never figured out what he was against because he never made it that far in his dialogue. Except once. The only time he managed to get his whole sentence out was at an old lady laden with grocery bags who couldn't walk fast enough to cut him off. She ignored him like a champion, though, and I was too engrossed in this turn of events to deconstruct his rapid Spanish sentence. Something about the upcoming elections. Reflections on Sevilla and Sevillanos

16 March 2015

   The Spanish are sensitive about their siestas. It seems to be a stereotype in Europe that the Spanish are lazy, and so they are quick to argue that they DO work hard, probably even harder than those other European countries. (This seems to be an especially sore spot with Germany.) If you innocently remark, upon being asked the differences between the US and Spain, that the Spanish way of life is more relaxed -- "No, the siesta makes us more productive!" - "It's healthier!" - "It's natural!".

    This weekend, too indecisive to commit to a weekend anywhere, I stayed in Sevilla - and it was great! On Friday I went to not one, not two, but three Sevillan museums I hadn't seen yet in these two months. The most interesting by far was the Spanish Inquisition museum, located underground in the ruins of the castle that was used as the second seat of the Inquisition in Sevilla. (As the Inquisition grew, the first seat was found to be too small. This first seat was also has the dubious honor of being the first seat of the Inquisition in all of Spain.)
   Maybe because it would be too hard to, maybe because they have no interest in doing so, but the Spanish really don't sugar-coat or try to defend the Inquisition at all, despite remaining a fairly Catholic country. The history books wax lyrical about convictions and burnings and every product of the Spanish education system is able to do the same. I appreciate this non-blind faith, though many aspects of the Catholicism practiced here are less orthodox than I expected (more on that later).

  Over the course of this weekend I also walked through four "mercadillos" - street markets. Every city/town has a day where the street markets appear, and in Sevilla it's Sunday and spills backward into Saturday. One of these markets took place in front of the Museum of Fine Arts and, as you might have guessed, was all art. Upon learning I was from America, one artist asked me to be his agent in America. I'm now armed with his business card and a calendar of samples of his work, so look out, Boston. Here is a link to his blog.

So I've been to all these places in the past two months, and I've liked all of them, unlikely and naively positive as that sounds. Chronologically:

Córdoba, Spain
Capital of Hispania during the Roman empire, and the Emirate and then Caliphate of Cordoba from the 8th to 11th century. In the 10th century it may have been the biggest city in the world, but today it is medium-sized, and felt medium small, in a friendly way.
Naturally, the Royal Palace was huge and luxurious.

Ronda, Spain
Scenery! Hiking! The city is called Ronda because it's suROuNDed(A) by mountains.

Also, this was the first place where I could really tell that there was an economic crisis in Spain. In Sevilla you don't notice it as much, maybe it's hidden behind the tourists, but in smaller and more isolated Ronda everything shuts down at 7 or 8 and the people look more stressed.

Granada, Spain
Last holdout of the Moors in Spain. The Muslim kings were in Granada for a good 200 years after the the Christian kings re-took the rest of Spain. This means that the royal palace became more of a fortress with a palace inside it. The fortress (the Alhambra) also contains a mosque, soldier's quarters, lookout towers, a park...
The whole place has some of the most complete Islamic architecture in Spain. Additionally, the Catholic Kings Isabel and Ferdinand are buried here, and you can see their caskets.
    Also, in Granada, they give you a free tapa (snack) with every drink you buy, which is pretty awesome.

Brussels and Bruges, Belgium
Waffles, beer, canals, unrealistic lions, Tintin

Faro, Portugal
2 March 2015

On Thursday, I made a last-minute decision to travel this weekend. On Friday, I got on a bus to Faro, Portugal, a town (with a beach!) 2.5 hours away. Faro is the capital of a region of the southern Portuguese coast called the Algarve, which is known for its cliffs and beaches.
I planned to get there on Friday afternoon and leave Saturday afternoon, and catch up on sleep back in Sevilla on Sunday.The day I got there, I wandered around among short white-walled buildings, the marina, some ruins, and "jardim"s -- on tourist maps, even the smallest parks are elevated to gardens.


The next day, I met three girls (one Australian, one German, and one Taiwanese) at the hostel's breakfast who also wanted to bike to the beach, and we agreed we'd go together. An hour into our ride, we were directed by a couple of returning bikers not to follow the road signs but to turn left at the next roundabout and go behind the airport. When we reached the next roundabout, there were three exits: to the right, straight ahead, and the way we had come. The sign for the beach pointed to the right, so obviously that wasn't it. The one straight ahead said it was to the airport, and I had visions of our little line of bikes stranded on the landing strip. But after chasing each other around the roundabout four times, the majority ruled that we would go straight. After some time we found ourselves on a single-bicycle-wide dirt path -- reassuring because it was clearly not a landing strip, concerning because what would you do if you actually did want to get to the airport? Eventually, we got to a beach and collapsed on the sand.


Time slowed down at this point. Naps were had by some, sandwiches by others (me). I spent some time trying to figure out the patterns in waves.
  Big waves crash and little waves emerge out of the leftovers, and fall over themselves trying to catch up to the big ones. Some parts of a wave never crash -- the falling on both sides gets to them first.
    A wave can't crash too far out or it'll never make it to shore, but if it tries to go to far in it finds sand under itself instead of water and nothing to make more wave out of, and it fizzles out.
    You can't crash too soon after the previous one, because you want to crash on top of it when it's almost done so it propels you forward and your momentum delays its withdrawal. But you can't crash too long after the previous one or you get caught by its withdrawal into the sea and don't get purchase on the shore.

The rocks I picked up on the beach were black and kind of aggressive-looking, which I thought was uncharacteristic of (the small bit of) Portugal that I'd experienced, sunny and friendly and relaxed.


Marruecos / Morocco

23 Feb. 2015

Last weekend, I went on a trip (excursion, actually) to Morocco. The excursion put us up in a super nice hotel that felt kinda insulating...but also was very nice. We ate dinner there the first night, and they had traditional dancers come in ( Sort of like this ).

My first impressions of Morocco outside of the hotel were on a bus ride to the city of Chefchaouen, and were mostly of the scenery. It was scenic - fog, classic rolling medium green hills that looked less touched by development/farming than those of Spain. The sheep, the goats, even the stray dogs seemed more liberated. It was also nice to see un-manicured grass, one thing I miss in Sevilla.

Chefchauen itself struck me as both exciting and remarkably peaceful. All the walls are painted blue to repel insects, and the doors of houses are color coded for the inhabitants - blue for Andalusian (Moors who left Spain), green and grey for Berbers (natives of Morocco), brown for Jews. The streets are too small for cars - instead, donkeys with 5-gallon tanks strapped to their backs plod up and down the brick/mud roads. The word Chefchaouen means (approximately) "look at the peaks," because the city is surrounded by three mountains. These loomed up behind the houses whenever you got close to the edges of the city. One of them is the origin of a stream that becomes a river and then a waterfall as it passes the city walls.


After lunch, we wandered up and down streets and between markets, working up the courage to haggle. Halfway through our collective first attempt, we realized that though our guides had taught us the rudiments of haggling, they hadn't given us the faintest idea of the going price for anything. We were reduced to guessing, and alternated between offending people with low offers and raising eyebrows with high ones. The statuettes, leather purses, scarves, bracelets, etc. were the same in every street stall, which made it easier.

Most of the transactions we carried out in Spanish. Some of the merchants spoke English, some didn't and only spoke Arabic, French, and Spanish. Speaking Spanish to the Moroccan merchants went more smoothly than it often does with Spaniards, because there was an understanding that this was a second language for both of us (well, my second; their third). The ones we hadn't already offended with too-low offers were friendly, happy to converse, and patient with our tenuous grasp on the haggling process.


  Very soon into our wanderings, it became hard to ignore an interesting phenomenon. Moroccans were really excited that I was Indian. Every fourth street vendor would spot me peering into stalls and exclaim "India!" And I would agree, taken aback by their enthusiasm and unsure of the proper response, and they would stop eveything they were doing to recite names of Bollywood actors.

    One guy listed off 7 (we both counted on our fingers), and one started singing and dancing. One bowed, raised his hands as for a namaste, and gravely said "Kuch Kuch Hota Hai". I passed one man and we had the conversation I was still confused by but by this point accustomed to; India--Si--Shahrukh Khan!--Si! I later passed him again and said "Shahrukh Khan!" and he grinned from ear to ear, high-fived me, and responded in kind "Amitabh Bhachchan!"

  I finally had time for a longer conversation with one of these India enthusiasts and asked him why Moroccans were such admirers of Shahrukh and Amitabh. Bollywood movies are always on tv, he said (in Spanish), very fun, very good for watching with the family. Hollywood movies are too violent and have "mucho sexo", but Bollywood movies, he smiled, are better, happier, and more suited to a Muslim country and values.
